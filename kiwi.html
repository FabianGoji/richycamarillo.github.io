01 — Context

An AR companion for one of travel's most stressful environments

Kiwi is an augmented reality airport navigation app designed for Interaction Design 2 at Kennesaw State University, taught by Professor Michael Lahey. Built as a team project for an AR operating system, Apple's Human Interface Guidelines informed our visual design decisions throughout — grounding the work in an established system rather than inventing conventions from scratch.

Airports are high-complexity, high-stress environments. Passengers routinely struggle to navigate terminals, interpret signage, locate gates, and find help quickly — especially in unfamiliar airports under time pressure. Kiwi was designed to reduce that friction by putting wayfinding, object recognition, staff assistance requests, and live maps and ticket information into a single AR-native interface that responds to the user's physical environment in real time.

"The airport is already a designed environment — Kiwi had to layer on top of it without adding to the noise."

02 — Process Foundation

Lean UX Canvas — structuring the problem before designing the solution

Before any design work began, the team used a Lean UX Canvas to establish shared alignment on the problem space. The canvas forced us to articulate business outcomes, user types, success metrics, and hypotheses before touching Figma — ensuring every design decision was grounded in clearly stated assumptions rather than intuition alone.

03 — Sprint 1

Mapping the core experience

Sprint 1 focused on establishing Kiwi's foundational flows: wayfinding, object recognition, and the assistance request system. Research into airport user behavior, AR interaction patterns, and Apple's Human Interface Guidelines formed the starting point — grounding early decisions in an established design system rather than convention-free invention.

My contributions centered on general research into AR UX patterns and airport navigation behavior, moderating two of the team's usability test sessions, and observing and recording notes across all sessions. Getting into a testing environment early — before designs were high-fidelity — meant assumptions were being validated while they were still cheap to change.

The central design challenges of Sprint 1 were AR overlay density — how much information could live in the camera view without overwhelming the user — and persistent UI placement, ensuring core controls stayed accessible without occluding the physical environment the user was navigating through.

Sprint 1 Retrospective — Usability testing surfaced two consistent gaps: the information hierarchy broke down in AR context when multiple overlays competed for attention, and several flows assumed too much interface familiarity for a first-time user in a live airport environment. Both became primary targets for Sprint 2.

04 — Sprint 2

Refining for real-world use

Sprint 2 responded directly to the retrospective findings. Flows were simplified, the assistance request system was made more prominent and immediately accessible, and maps and ticket information were integrated more naturally into the wayfinding context — rather than treated as separate destinations requiring additional navigation steps.

My primary contribution in Sprint 2 was prototyping the maps experience — designing both the visual elements and the interaction flow for how a user transitions from the live AR camera view into a schematic terminal map and back. I also contributed to defining Kiwi's brand style, which drew from Apple's design language: clean surfaces, high contrast for legibility across varied lighting conditions, and a restrained color system that wouldn't compete with the live camera environment.

Apple's HIG addresses AR-specific legibility constraints explicitly, and applying those guidelines pushed us toward higher contrast ratios and simpler iconography than our initial designs assumed — a refinement that testing in the second sprint validated.

Sprint 2 Retrospective — The second retrospective examined what remained unresolved within the academic timeline — primarily the object recognition flow, which would benefit from another iteration with more detailed edge-case testing, and the assistance request system, which would need live network integration to validate in a real airport environment. Both are areas I'd prioritize in a third sprint.

Reflections

What designing for AR taught me

Designing for AR introduced constraints I hadn't encountered before — legibility in a live camera environment, the relationship between digital overlays and physical space, and designing interactions for a user whose hands and attention are already occupied navigating a terminal. Standard UI design frameworks don't fully prepare you for any of those.

Apple's HIG gave us a rigorous starting point, but applying established guidelines to a specific AR context required interpretive decisions the documentation couldn't make for us. That gap between a design system and a particular use case is where the most interesting design thinking happens — and Kiwi put me directly in it.

Moderating and observing usability tests at the pace of a two-sprint project also sharpened how I structure observation notes and translate findings into actionable design changes quickly. The feedback loop between testing and iteration was the most valuable part of the process.
