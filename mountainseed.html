01 — Context

Expanding an analytics platform to reach bank lenders

MountainSeed is an appraisal management and analytics platform built for commercial real estate professionals. When I joined for a two-month internship, the product team was planning a significant expansion: extending the analytics platform to reach bank lenders — a new user type with distinct workflows and expectations.

The project was called the "Lender Portfolio". The goal was to give portfolio managers, credit analysts, and loan officers a consolidated space to manage and analyze their collateral properties — something that, until now, the platform had not offered.

"Give lenders a consolidated space for collateral properties and the data visualizations that communicate the metrics that inform strategy, append reports, and support ongoing loan inquiries."

02 — Research

Building fluency in a completely new domain

Walking into a Fintech company with no background in commercial real estate or appraisal management meant a steep learning curve before any design work could begin. Research wasn't just a phase — it was a continuous thread throughout the entire project.

The first priority was establishing CRE fundamentals, with a particular emphasis on the role of appraisers, appraisals, and the life cycle of a commercial loan. This combined external reading with internal deep-dives into historical artifacts and studies MountainSeed had already conducted.

  • 1
    Public CRE market reports & appraisal documents Commercial and residential appraisals, market reports, and industry literature to establish foundational CRE context.
  • 2
    Internal interview recordings & usability test transcripts Prior sessions conducted by the analytics team that surfaced lender behaviors, pain points, and mental models.
  • 3
    Internal lender study reports Research compiled by the team on the lender user type — attitudes, end goals, and behaviors relevant to product design.
  • 4
    Meeting with Product Manager Rosha Caines A structured session to learn the evolution of analytics at MountainSeed, the lender user type, and the current product vision — prepared with targeted questions in advance.
  • 5
    Lender role breakdown from mentor Bethany Stolle A comprehensive reference document on bank lender roles that informed design decisions throughout the entire project lifecycle.

03 — Define

Scoping the feature and selecting target users

With a solid foundation in the domain, the next step was translating research into a concrete design direction. Working with Bethany and Rosha, we defined the feature requirements, identified key workflows, and decided which lender roles to target first.

"Credit analysts were primary — their workflows aligned most closely with existing appraiser tools and opened up MountainSeed's assets in an original, lucrative way."

Two roles were selected: Credit Analysts as primary, and Loan Officers / Relationship Bankers as secondary. Credit analysts surfaced the most compelling overlap with appraiser workflows, while loan officers had clearer, more defined needs that could be faithfully served without additional exploration.

Research pointed to three things the portfolio tab needed to provide: comparative market analysis, collateral properties analysis, and property organization and accessibility. Seven use-case scenarios were written — two designated as key scenarios — giving the team a narrative grounding for every major design decision.

  • A
    Comparative Market Analysis Visualizations generated from MountainSeed's proprietary data, showing collateral properties in the context of the surrounding market — providing insight into trends, density, and relative position.
  • B
    Collateral Properties Analysis Portfolio-level metrics and charts providing insight into a bank's own property performance — informing strategy and supporting ongoing loan inquiries.
  • C
    Property Organization & Accessibility A searchable, filterable, sortable property system that gives lenders fast, precise discovery and access to any collateral record.

04 — Design

From hand-drawn sketches to high-fidelity prototype

Design began with hand-drawn sketches — a deliberate choice to keep early exploration fast and low-commitment. Sketching multiple interface variations for each flow made it easy to test information architecture ideas without getting attached to visual polish.

Moving into Figma, the first wireframe iteration kept fidelity low while referencing MountainSeed's existing element styles to maintain brand consistency. The layout placed all three portfolio pillars — map, data analysis, and properties — in a single consolidated space, with a shared filter set that applied across all three simultaneously.

Weekly syncs with the analytics team — spanning Technology, Data Ops, and Project Management — kept design tightly coupled to what was feasible and what the data could actually support. Each week brought new findings, which meant continuous iteration.

The final high-fidelity prototype brought together the confirmed visualizations, an updated properties table reflecting the latest data availability, and two new additions: a record flagging system with a side-sheet ticket queue, and an inquiry request workflow with error-state messaging for data visibility limits. A card-view alternative to the default table format was also produced.

05 — Data Visualization

A design thread that ran the full project lifecycle

Data visualization wasn't a single deliverable — it was an ongoing problem that evolved as the team learned more about what was technically possible. Defining the right charts required fluency in three overlapping areas: data analytics principles, visualization tool capabilities, and MountainSeed's available property data.

I met with Data Ops to understand what data existed, how it was structured, and what calculations it could support. I cross-referenced this against public data analytics principles and the documentation for the team's chosen visualization technology to identify a viable set of visual types. These were documented and reported to the team — a living reference that was revised as tech capabilities became clearer week by week.

"The challenge wasn't what to visualize — it was identifying which visualizations could be faithfully produced with our data and our tools, without overpromising."

The conclusion was a set of visualizations illustrating trends, distribution, and density — each tied directly to a data type MountainSeed could reliably extract. A known challenge was that different banks value different metrics, making a universal default layout nearly impossible without customization — a problem flagged explicitly for future iterations.

06 — Handoff & Support

From prototype to production — a shift into a support role

Once the prototype screens were finalized, the role shifted from leading design to supporting development. This meant being available for developer inquiries, providing revised specs when element dimensions or spacing needed adjustment during implementation, and rigorously testing implemented pages as soon as they were available.

QA findings were documented in structured reports — organized by area, date, and sub-section — with explicit notes even for areas that passed review, to demonstrate thoroughness to the project manager. The goal was always easy digestion: a developer or PM should be able to open a document and immediately find what they need.

Reflections

What I took away

This internship compressed a full product design lifecycle into two months — research, definition, ideation, wireframing, hi-fi design, prototyping, and production support. Working in a fast-moving Fintech environment with a cross-functional team taught me how to stay grounded in user needs while continuously adapting to technical and business constraints.

The most valuable skill I built was knowing when to push for clarity and when to design for ambiguity — particularly around data visualization, where the gap between what's desirable and what's producible is constantly shifting.
