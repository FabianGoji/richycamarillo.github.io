01 — The Problem

Young adults want to cook — the barrier isn't recipes

Young adults in transition — moving out for the first time, adjusting to tighter budgets, managing their own schedules — often lack both the cooking experience and the motivation to develop it. The problem isn't a shortage of recipes online. It's that existing tools assume a baseline of knowledge and confidence that many young adults simply don't have.

Ladle was designed to meet users where they are, not where the internet assumes they are. The core question was: what does an app look like when it treats cooking confidence as the primary product, not recipe delivery?

"The problem isn't access to recipes — it's confidence, motivation, and not knowing what to do with what's already in the kitchen."

02 — Methodology

Goal-Directed Design — grounding decisions in user goals

Goal-Directed Design, developed by Alan Cooper, is a methodology that grounds every design decision in a deep understanding of the user's goals, behaviors, and motivations — rather than starting from features or technology. The process moves through five phases, each building on the last.

As team lead, I was responsible for driving the GDD process, facilitating research synthesis, defining the design framework, and keeping the team's work connected to the original user goals throughout. We completed all phases with the exception of cross-departmental knowledge transfer — which applies to production handoff in a real product context and was not applicable in an academic setting.

Research
Investigating user behaviors, goals, and motivations through primary and secondary research. This phase produced the raw material for persona development and identified the core problem space.
Modeling
Synthesizing research into behavioral personas — representing the distinct user types and their goals, frustrations, and mental models. Personas grounded in research, not assumptions.
Requirements Definition
Translating persona goals into design requirements — identifying what the product must do to serve each user type. This phase connected user research directly to design decisions.
Framework Design
Defining the overall structure of the product — information architecture, interaction patterns, and the core feature set — mapped directly to user goals rather than assumed functionality.
Refinement
Iterating on the framework with increasing fidelity — moving from wireframes toward a prototype, resolving layout, interaction, and visual design decisions.

03 — Where Focus Drifted

An honest account of scope drift

Ladle started with a clear and specific target user — young adults with low cooking experience and motivation. As the project progressed, the team tried to accommodate a broader audience, and the scope expanded beyond what the core feature set could faithfully serve. The result was a weaker main tool — a product that tried to speak to everyone and ended up being less precise for anyone.

Retrospective

It was my first project leading a team through a rigorous methodology, and the lesson was hard-earned: a focused problem statement is not a limitation — it's a foundation. Without it, every subsequent design decision becomes harder to defend, and the methodology itself loses its anchor.

Goal-Directed Design gave us the right process. What I'd do differently is hold the scope boundaries more firmly, earlier — and use the methodology's own outputs (the personas, the scenarios, the requirements) as the check against any feature or audience expansion that threatened to dilute the original user focus.

Reflections

What I carried forward

Ladle taught me more than any project where everything went according to plan. The experience of watching a well-defined problem gradually dilute under scope pressure — and not catching it soon enough — is something I've carried into every project since.

Using Goal-Directed Design as a methodology was a genuine strength. GDD is a rigorous framework and knowing how to apply it — even imperfectly on a first attempt — builds a kind of design thinking that doesn't come from easier projects. The retrospective is where Ladle earns its place in this portfolio: not as a polished outcome, but as the project that most clearly shaped how I work now.

It was my first project. It won't be my last chance to get it right.
