01 — Context

A personal passion turned into a user-centered design

Find Your Ride started from a simple observation: skateboarding has a steep entry barrier for beginners — not because the sport is inaccessible, but because the equipment decisions alone are overwhelming. Decks, trucks, wheels, bushings, bearings — each component affects how a board feels and performs, and most beginners have no framework for making informed choices.

As a skater, I had that framework. Find Your Ride was designed to give beginners one too — built as a web prototype for User Interface Design 2 at KSU, and grounded in twenty UI design principles applied as deliberate design decisions rather than academic checkboxes.

"Being a skater made me a better designer on this project — I had an internal reference point for whether every decision actually served a beginner trying to learn."

02 — The Content

What Find Your Ride teaches

The prototype guides users through the board selection process by covering different skating styles — street, park, cruising, longboarding — and explaining how each style maps to specific component choices. Users leave with a clear understanding of what kind of board fits their goals and why, rather than a generic recommendation with no rationale behind it.

The content covers skating styles and their distinct demands, board components — deck shape and size, truck width and height, wheel hardness and diameter, and hardware — and the relationship between a rider's experience level and the board that will serve them best. Every section was written from personal knowledge and structured to build on the last, creating a learning arc rather than a disconnected reference page.

03 — Design Decisions

UI principles applied as design rationale

Twenty UI principles informed the design — four applied with particular intentionality. Rather than treating them as a checklist, each principle drove a specific design decision in service of the user.

Multimedia Principle
Component diagrams were paired with written descriptions for every part covered. Skateboard terminology — trucks, bushings, hardness ratings — means nothing to a beginner without a visual reference. Text and image had to work together, not independently.
Spatial Contiguity
Related content was positioned in close physical proximity throughout the layout, reducing the cognitive load of connecting a component name to its diagram and its function. Information that belongs together was placed together.
Von Restorff Effect
Key recommendations and critical distinctions were visually differentiated from surrounding content — ensuring the most actionable information registered rather than disappearing into a dense page.
Chunking
Content was broken into digestible sections by skating style and component category, making a complex topic feel navigable rather than overwhelming. A beginner doesn't need to know everything at once — they need to know the right thing at the right moment.

04 — What's Next

A board recommendation tool — the next version

The retrospective surfaced an idea I haven't let go of: a board recommendation tool that factors in a user's body stats, preferred skating environment, and experience goals to generate a personalized component list. It's a natural evolution of the prototype's educational premise into something more interactive and tailored.

Where Find Your Ride gives a beginner the framework to understand their options, the recommendation tool would close the loop — taking that understanding and translating it into a specific, justified starting setup. It's something I intend to build.

Reflections

On designing something you already know

FYR is the most personal project in my portfolio. Designing something I'm genuinely knowledgeable about changed how I approached every decision — I had an internal reference point for whether the content was accurate, whether the structure made sense, and whether a beginner would actually understand what they were reading.

That proximity to the subject matter made me a better designer on this project. It's something I want to replicate wherever possible — bringing real domain knowledge into design work, rather than designing from the outside looking in.
